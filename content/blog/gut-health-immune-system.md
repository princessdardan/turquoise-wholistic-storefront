The human gut is home to trillions of microorganisms — bacteria, fungi, and viruses — collectively known as the microbiome. Far from being passive passengers, these tiny inhabitants play a critical role in your overall health, particularly your immune function.

## The Gut-Immune Axis

Research has revealed that approximately 70% of the immune system resides in the gut-associated lymphoid tissue (GALT). This makes the digestive tract the body's largest immune organ and the frontline of defence against pathogens.

Your gut microbiome supports immunity in several ways:

- **Barrier function** — beneficial bacteria strengthen the intestinal lining, preventing harmful substances from entering the bloodstream
- **Immune modulation** — gut microbes communicate directly with immune cells, helping calibrate the immune response
- **Pathogen competition** — a diverse, healthy microbiome crowds out harmful bacteria by competing for resources and space

## Supporting Your Microbiome

### Probiotics

Probiotics are live beneficial bacteria that can be taken as supplements or found in fermented foods. Look for strains with clinical evidence:

- *Lactobacillus rhamnosus GG* — one of the most studied probiotic strains
- *Bifidobacterium longum* — supports gut barrier integrity
- *Saccharomyces boulardii* — a beneficial yeast that supports digestive health during travel

### Prebiotics

Prebiotics are the fibre-rich foods that feed your beneficial bacteria. Great sources include:

- Garlic and onions
- Asparagus and artichokes
- Bananas (especially slightly green ones)
- Oats and flaxseeds

### Fermented Foods

Traditional fermented foods provide both probiotics and unique bioactive compounds:

- Sauerkraut and kimchi
- Kefir and yogourt
- Kombucha
- Miso and tempeh

## What Harms Your Microbiome?

Be mindful of factors that can disrupt your gut ecosystem:

1. **Unnecessary antibiotics** — while sometimes essential, antibiotics are indiscriminate and kill beneficial bacteria along with harmful ones
2. **Highly processed foods** — refined sugars and artificial additives can favour harmful bacterial strains
3. **Chronic stress** — the gut-brain axis means stress directly impacts microbial balance
4. **Poor sleep** — disrupted circadian rhythms affect the microbiome's own daily cycles

## Building a Gut-Healthy Routine

Start with whole, plant-rich foods as the foundation. Add one or two fermented foods daily, consider a high-quality probiotic supplement, and manage stress through practices like meditation, yoga, or time in nature. Your gut — and your immune system — will thank you.
